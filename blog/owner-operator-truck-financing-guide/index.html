The 5-step approach

Step 1: Get the real numbers first

Pull industry benchmarks for your category before writing a single section. For trucking, that means fuel runs 38¢ of every revenue dollar (atri 2025). Trade publications and federal data (BLS, SBA, IBISWorld) are starting points, but the actual delta is in operator interviews.

Step 2: Match capital to model

Trucking businesses scale on three levers: capital, capacity, and recurring revenue. Solo owner-operators and team drivers typically run into trouble when they pick the wrong lever first. Decide:

  • How much cash can you commit in months 1-12?
  • How much do you need to keep in reserve (working capital)?
  • Which model — small/home vs full retail/center — matches the gap?

Step 3: Build the financial model

Open a spreadsheet. List every monthly cost: rent, utilities, insurance, equipment lease, payroll, marketing, working capital reserve. Sum them. That is your break-even revenue.

Now back-calculate: how many customers, jobs, or units do you need to hit it? If the answer is “more than your market can support,” you have a model problem, not a marketing problem.

The 137-page trucking playbook does this math automatically with editable inputs — but you can prototype it in a single afternoon.

Step 4: Pressure-test with the lender lens

If you’d send this plan to an SBA lender tomorrow, what would they ask? Common red flags:

  • No working capital reserve (3 months minimum)
  • Margins above industry norm with no explanation
  • Revenue ramp that ignores seasonality
  • Missing or hand-waved competition section

A plan that survives the lender lens also survives Year 2.

Step 5: Execute on a 90-day timeline

Compress the first 90 days into specific, dated milestones:

  • Days 1-30: Foundation — entity formation, insurance quotes, location shortlist, equipment shortlist.
  • Days 31-60: Soft commitments — LOI on space, vendor agreements, financing applications.
  • Days 61-90: Launch prep — final lease/equipment, marketing soft-open, first 10 customers.

If you miss a milestone by more than 14 days, stop and re-scope. Don’t push through; the plan needs re-grounding.

Common pitfalls (and how to avoid them)

  1. Trusting one source. Owner Operator Truck Financing guides vary wildly. Pull from at least 3 independent sources before committing to a number.
  2. Skipping the lender pressure-test. Even if you’re self-financing, run the plan as if a stranger has to approve it. Discipline > optimism.
  3. No working capital reserve. Year 1 is when cash gaps kill businesses. Plan for 3 months operating expenses sitting in a separate account.
  4. Confusing revenue with profit. Top-line numbers don’t matter if the unit economics break. Fuel runs 38¢ of every revenue dollar (ATRI 2025) — but only when the operating model is disciplined.
  5. Going alone. real CPM calculator, MC authority roadmap, and 36-month P&L are available. Use them.
